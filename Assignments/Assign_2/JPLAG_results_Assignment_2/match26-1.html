Solution 1
~~~~~~~~~~

First, we establish an identity for the Vandermonde determinants:

.. math::

\left|\matrix
{
1 & x_{0} & x_{0}^{2} & \ldots & x_{0}^{n-1} &
x_{0}^{n}\\
1 & x_{1} & x_{1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{1}^{n-1} &
x_{1}^{n} \\
1 & x_{2} & x_{2}^{2} & \ldots & x_{2}^{n-1} &
x_{2}^{n} \\
1 & x_{3} & x_{3}^{2} & \ldots & x_{3}^{n-1} & x_{3}^{n}\\
\
vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
1 & x_{n} & x_{n}^{2} & \ldots &
x_{n}^{n-1}& x_{n}^{n} \\ }\right| = \prod_{0\leq i<j\leq n}(x_{j}-x_{i})

The proof is by induction on the size n of the determinant. For n=1 the
claim is true by default. Then with the next value n=2:

.. math:: \left|\matrix{
1 & x_{0} \\ 1 & x_{1} \\ }\right| = x_{1}-x_{0}

Now for the inductive step, let us assume

.. math:: \left|\matrix{
1 & x_{0} & x_{0}^{2} & \ldots & x_{0}^{k}\\
1 & x_{1} & x_{1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{1}^{k} \\
1 & x_{2} & x_{2}^{2} & \ldots & x_{2}^{k} \\
1 & x_{3} & x_{3}^{2} & \ldots & x_{3}^{k} \\ \
vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & x_{k} & x_{k}^{2} & \ldots & x_{k}^{k} \\ }\right| = \prod_{1\leq i<j\leq k+1}(x_{j}-x_{i})

and let n=k+1. Consider,

.. math::

\Delta=\left|\matrix
{
1 & x_{0} & x_{0}^{2} & \ldots & x_{0}^{k+
1}\\
1 & x_{1} & x_{1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{1}^{k+
1} \\
1 & x_{2} & x_{2}^{2} & \ldots & x_{2}^{k+
1} \\
1 & x_{3} & x_{3}^{2} & \ldots & x_{3}^{k+1} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
1 & x_{k+1} & x_{k+1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{k+1}^{k+1} \\ }\right|

First we subtract the last row in turn from all others:

.. math::

\Delta=\left|\matrix{ 0 & x_{0}-x_{k+
1} & x_{0}^{2}-x_{k+1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{0}^{k}-x_{k+1}^{k} & x_{0}^{k+1}-x_{k+1}^{k+1} \\
0 & x_{1}-x_{k+
1} & x_{1}^{2}-x_{k+1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{1}^{k}-x_{k+1}^{k} & x_{1}^{k+1}-x_{k+1}^{k+1} \\
0 & x_{2}-x_{k+
1} & x_{2}^{2}-x_{k+1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{2}^{k}-x_{k+1}^{k} & x_{2}^{k+1}-x_{k+1}^{k+1} \\
0 & x_{3}-x_{k+
1} & x_{3}^{2}-x_{k+1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{3}^{k}-x_{k+1}^{k} & x_{3}^{k+1}-x_{k+1}^{k+1} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\
1 & x_{k+1} & x_{k+1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{k+1}^{k} & x_{k+1}^{k+1} \\ }\right|

Next, multiply column k+1 by :math:`x_{k+1}` and subtract the result
from column k+2; multiply column k by :math:`x_k` and subtract the
result from column k+1, and so on:

.. math::

\Delta=\left|\matrix
{ 0 & x_{0}-x_{k+1} & x_{0}(x_{0}-x_{k+1}) & \ldots & x_{0}^{k-1}(x_{0}-x_{k+1}) & x_{0}^{k}(x_{0}-x_{k+1})\\
0 & x_{1}-x_{k+1} & x_{1}(x_{1}-x_{k+1}) & \ldots & x_{1}^{k-1}(x_{1}-x_{k+1}) & x_{1}^{k}(x_{1}-x_{k+1}) \\
0 & x_{2}-x_{k+1} & x_{2}(x_{2}-x_{k+1}) & \ldots & x_{2}^{k-1}(x_{2}-x_{k+1}) & x_{2}^{k}(x_{2}-x_{k+1}) \\
0 & x_{3}-x_{k+1} & x_{3}(x_{3}-x_{k+1}) & \ldots & x_{3}^{k-1}(x_{3}-x_{k+1}) & x_{3}^{k}(x_{3}-x_{k+1}) \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\
1 & 0 & 0 & \ldots & 0 & 0 \\ }\right|

Expanding the determinant by the first column gives

.. math::

\Delta=(-1)^{k+3}\prod_{0\leq i\leq k+1}(x_{i}-x_{k+1})
\left|\matrix
{
1 & x_{0} & x_{0}^{2} & \ldots & x_{1}^{k} \\
1 & x_{1} & x_{1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{1}^{k} \\
1 &
x_{2} & x_{2}^{2} & \ldots & x_{2}^{k} \\
1 &
x_{3} & x_{3}^{2} & \ldots & x_{3}^{k} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
1 & x_{k} & x_{k}^{2} & \ldots & x_{k}^{k} \\ }
\right|=
\prod_{0\leq i\leq k+1}(x_{k+1}-x_{i})
\left|\matrix
{ 1 &
x_{0} & x_{0}^{2} & \ldots & x_{0}^{k} \\
1 &
x_{1} & x_{1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{1}^{k} \\
1 & x_{2} & x_{2}^{2} & \ldots & x_{2}^{k} \\
1 & x_{3} & x_{3}^{2} & \ldots & x_{3}^{k} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
1 & x_{k+1} & x_{k+1}^{2} & \ldots & x_{k+1}^{k} \\ }\right|

which together with mathematical induction completes the proof.

i.e. determinant of Vandermonde matrix of order n+1 is
:math:`\prod_{0 \le i < j \le n} (x_j - x_i)`

on expanding

.. math:: \left(\prod_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-x_0)\right)\left(\prod_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-x_1)\right)\left(\prod_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-x_2)\right) \cdots \left(\prod_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-x_{n-1})\right)

Now keeping the determinant in mind, we can easily answer that,

a. Determinant is polynomial of degree :
math:`\frac{n(n+1)}{2}` with
variables :math:`x_0,x_1,x_2\dots x_n`.

b. from the expression we can see that, if :
math:`x_i=x_j` for any
:math:`i\ne j`, detterminant will be 0.

c. every :
math:`(x_i-x_j)` is a factor of the determinant for
:math:`i\ne j`.

d. Determinant of :math:`Vandermonde` matrix of order n+1 is
:math:`\prod_{0 \le i < j \le n} (x_j - x_i)` =
:math:`C\left(\prod_{0 \le j < i \le n} (
x_i - x_j)\right)`

where value of :math:`C` has two options, 1 or -1.

e.

consider the highest degree term then we have,

.. math:: x_1x_2^2x_3^3x_4^4\dots x_n^n

, which have coefficiant equal to 1, thus it is clearly seen that
:math:`C=1`.

Solution 2
~~~~~~~~~~

We need
to show that the Legendre polynomials satisfy the recurrence

.. math:: q_{n+1} = xq_n - \left({\dfrac{n^2}{4n^2-1}}\right)q_{n-1}

In
L.H.S :math:`q_{n+1}`, On multiplying by it :math:`q_n` and integrate
over -1 to 1, which gives

..
math:: \int_{-1}^1 q_n(x) q_{n+1}(x)dx = 0

by orthogonality
property.

Now consider R.H.S. i.e.
:
math:`xq_n - \left({\dfrac{n^2}{4n^2-1}}\right)q_{n-1}`, again
multiplying by it :
math:`q_n(x)` and integrate
over -1 to 1 ,we get

.. raw:: latex

\begin{align}
&\int_{-1}^1 xq_n^2-\left({\dfrac{n^2}{4n^2-1}}\right)q_{n-1}q_ndx\\
&\int_{-1}^1 xq_n^2dx-\int_{-1}^1\left({\dfrac{n^2}{4n^2-1}}\right)q_{n-1}q_ndx
\end{align}

second term is equal to zero by orthogonality condition, for first term

..
raw:: latex

\begin{align}
\int_{-1}^1 xq_n^2dx &= \left(x \frac{2}{2n+1}\right)_{-1}^1- \int_{-1}^1 1.\frac{2}{2n+1} dx\\
&=\left(x \frac{2}{2n+1}\right)_{-1}^1-\left(x \frac{2}{2n+1}\right)_{-1}^1\\
&=0
\end{align}

First term is equal to zero by
using\ :
math:`\int_{-1}^1 q_n(x)^2dx = \frac{2}{2n+1}`.

thus L.H.S = R.H.S from which we conclude that
legendre polynomial
satisfies the given recurrence.

2.b.

We are given that :math:`p(x)` is a monic polynomial of degree :math:`n`
so that we assume that :math:`p(x)=q_n(x)+r(x)` where :math:`q_n(x)` is
legendre polynomial of degree n and :math:`r(
x)` is a polynomial of
degree :math:`n-1` (which can be written as sum of legendre polynomials
of degrees less than :math:`n`.)

.. raw:: latex

\begin{align}
\int_{-1}^1 p^2(x)dx &= \int_{-1}^1 {(q_n(x)+r(x))}^2 dx\\
&= \int_{-1}^{1} q^2_n(x)+r(x)^2+2q_n(x)r(x) dx\\
\end{align}

Now, by orthogonality condition the last term i.e.
:math:`\int_{-1}^12q_n(x)r(x) dx =0`.

To minimize :math:`p(x)` , $:raw-latex:`\int`\_{-1}^1 r(x)^2 dx $ should
equal to zero . therefore

.. math:: \int_{-1}^1 p(x)^2 dx= \int_{-1}^{1} q_n(x)^2 dx

thus :math:`p(x)=q_n(x)`.

Since, the given expression
:math:`\int_{-1}^{1} \left(\prod_{k=0}^{n} (x-x_k)\right)^2`, is a monic
polynomial of degree n, from above we can conclude that :math:`x_k` as
legendre's nodes minimize the given polynomial.

Solution 3
~~~~~~~~~~

3.a. The Chebyshev polynomials of the first kind are defined as:

.. math:: T_n(x) = \cos(n \arccos(x))

From the L.H.S.

..
raw:: latex

\begin{align}
T_{n+1}(x) &= \cos((n+1) \arccos(x))\\
&=\cos(n\arccos(x)+\arccos(x))\\
&= \cos(n\arccos(x))(\cos(\arccos(x)))-\sin(n\arccos(x))(\sin(\arccos(x)))\\
&= xT_n(x)-\sin(n\arccos(x))\sin(\arccos(x))\\
xT_n(x)-T_{n+1}(x)&=\sin(n\arccos(x))\sin(\arccos(x))
\end{align}

Now from the R.H.S.

..
raw:: latex

\begin{align}
T_{n-1}(x) &= \cos((n-1) \arccos(x))\\
&=\cos(n\arccos(x)-\arccos(x))\\
&= \cos(n\arccos(x))(\cos(\arccos(x)))+\sin(n\arccos(x))(\sin(\arccos(x)))\\
&= xT_n(x)+\sin(n\arccos(x))\sin(\arccos(x))\\
T_{n-1}(x)-xT_n(x)&=\sin(n\arccos(x))\sin(\arccos(x))
\end{align}

From the above two equations, we can conclude:

..
raw:: latex

\begin{align}
xT_n(x)-T_{n+1}(x)&=T_{n-1}(x)-xT_n(x)\\
T_{n+1} &= 2xT_n - T_{n-1}
\end{align}

3.b. We have
the recurrence relation

.. math:: T_{n+1} = 2xT_n - T_{n-1}

we prove above statement by induction method-
for :math:`n=1`,

.. math:: T_{2} = 2xT_1 - T_{0}

.. math:: T_{2} = 2x^2 -1

since :
math:`T_0 = 1` and :math:`T_1 = x`.

cleary it is satisfied .

Now assume that given statement is true
for chebyshev polynomial of
degree :math:`k`, and check for polynomial of degree :math:`k+1` Again
consider the recurrence relation

.. math:: T_{k+1} = 2xT_k - T_{k-1}

By assumption :math:`T_k(
x)` is a polynomial of degree :math:`k`
with
leading coefficient as :math:`2^{k-1}` for :math:`k \geq 1` and
:math:`T_{k-
1}` is a polynomial of degree :math:`k-
1` with leading
coefficient as :math:`2^{k-2}` for :math:`k \geq 1`.

Hence for :math:`T_k(
x)`, the leading term comes from
on multiplying
:math:`2x` with :math:`2^{k-1}x^k` which is :math:`2^k x^{k+1}`,
completing the proof.

3.c.
To find zeros of :math:`T_{n+1}(x)`,

..
raw:: latex

\begin{align}
T_{n+1}(x)= \cos((n+1)\arccos(x))&=0\\
(n+1)\arccos(x)&= (2k+1)\frac{\pi}{2}\\
\arccos(x)&= \frac{2k+1}{2n+2}\pi\\
x_k&=\cos{\left(\dfrac{2k+1}{2n+2}\right)\pi}
\end{align}

Here, :
math:`k\in (0,1,2\cdots, n)` which is :
math:`n+1` points i.e. the
polymonial
cuts :math:`x` axis at :math:`n+1` points.

3.d. From above, we can
conclude that :math:`T_n(x)` alternates between
:math:`\pm1` exactly :math:`n+1` times.

3.e. We can see
that :math:`|{\prod_{k=0}^n (x-x_k})|` is monic
polynomial where :
math:`x_k` are Chebyshev nodes. But the Chebyshev
polynomial :
math:`T_{n+1}(x)` is
not monic since it has leading
coefficient as :
math:`2^n`.

Thus to make it monic, we divide the Chebysheve polynomial by
:math:`2^n`.
We get,

.. math:: {\prod_{k=0}^n (x-x_k})=\frac{T_{n+1}(x)}{2^n}

on taking mod both sides,

..
math:: |{\prod_{k=0}^n (x-x_k})|=|\frac{T_{n+1}(x)}{2^n}|

For the maximum,

..
math:: \max_{[-1,1]}|{T_{n+1}(x)}| =\max_{[-1,1]}|\cos((n+1) \arccos(x))|\leq 1

therefore,

.. math:: |{\prod_{k=0}^n (x-x_k})|\leq \frac{1}{2^n}

3.f. Given :math:`P_{n+1}(x)` is monic polynomial of degree :math:`n+1`
having :math:`(y_k)` $ k :raw-latex:`\in `0,1,2,:raw-latex:`\cdots `n$
as its roots. Also, :math:`\frac{T_{n+1}(x)}{2^n}` is monic Chebyshev
polynomial of degree :math:`n+1` and it has
:math:`x_k=\cos{\left(\frac{2k+1}{2n+2}\right)\pi}` as its roots.

Now both polynomials are monic and so when we take their difference thus
the leading term vanishes, making :math:`F(x)`
a polynomial of degree
:math:`n`.

Now, to show that :math:`F(x)` alternates in sign :math:`n+2` on
:math:`[-1,1]`, we find the roots of :math:`F(x)`, which gives us

.. math:: P_{n+1}(x) = \frac{T_{n+1}(x)}{2^n}

.. math:: \prod_{k=0}^n (x-y_k)=\prod_{k=0}^n (x-x_k)

This is only possible when both :math:`x_k` and :math:`y_k` are same.

Thus :math:`P_n(x)=\frac{T_{n+1}(x)}{2^n}` and :math:`F(x)` have
:math:`n+1` roots but :math:`F(x)` is polynomial of degree :math:`n`.
From this, we conclude that :math:`F(x)` is identically zero.

Which implies that Chebyshev nodes minimizes
:math:`\max_{x \in [-1,1]}|\prod_{k=0}^n ({x-x_k})|`.

Solution 5

Given

.. raw:: latex

\begin{align}
\sum_{j=0}^n x_j^m \ell_j(x) = x^m
\end{align}

let's assume there is p(x) polynomial such that

..
raw:: latex

\begin{align}
p(x)=\sum_{j=0}^n x_j^m \ell_j(x)
\end{align}

As
we know that , given any :math:`f(x)` , we can interpolate
with the
help of lagrange polynomial. i.e.

.. raw:: latex

\begin{align}
f(x) \approx p(x)= \sum_{j=0}^n f_j \ell_j(x)
\end{align}
