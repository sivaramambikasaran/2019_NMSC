**:math:`2.`** - Monic Legendre polynomials on :math:`[-1,1]` are
defined as, :math:`q_0(x)=1` & :math:`q_1(x)=x`.   :math:`q_n(x)` is a
monic polynomial of degree :math:`n` such that
:math:`\int_{-1}^1q_n(x)q_m(x)dx=0 \forall m \ne n`   :math:`\therefore`
The weight function :math:`w(x)=1`.   Now if :math:`u` & :math:`v` are
:math:`n` times differentiable on :math:`[-1,1]` and :math:`v` & its
first :math:`(n-1)` derivatives are zero at both endpoints
:math:`x=\pm 1`, then :math:`\forall n\geq 1`\    
:math:`\int_{-1}^1u(x)v^{(n)}(x)dx=(-1)^n\int_{-1}^1u^{(n)}(x)v(x)dx`\
  Now if :math:`v_n=\frac{d^n}{dx^n}[(x^2-1)^n]`, we get,  
:math:`v_n=\frac{d^n}{dx^n}`
:math:`[\sum_{k=0}^n \left(\begin{array}{c} n \\ k\end{array}\right)(-1)^kx^{2n-2k}]`\ =\ :math:`[\sum_{k=0}^n \left(\begin{array}{c} n \\ k\end{array}\right)(-1)^k \frac{d^n}{dx^n}x^{2n-2k}]`\ =\ :math:`\frac{(2n)!}{n!}x^n-n\frac{(2n-2)!}{(n-2)!}x^{n-2} \pm .....(1)`\
  Hence we see that,  
:math:`q_n=x^n- \frac{n(n-1)}{2(2n-1)}x^{n-2} \pm .....(2)`\   Since
:math:`w(x)=1` is odd, we have the three terms recurrence, given by,  
:math:`q_{n+1}=xq_n- \beta_n q_{n-1}`\   Comparing the coefficient of
:math:`x^{n-}` from the above relation & :math:`(2)`, we obtain  
:math:`\frac{n(n+1)}{2(2n+1)}=-\frac{n(n-1)}{2(2n-1)}- \beta_n`\   This
gives that, :math:`\beta_n=\frac{n^2}{4n^2-1}`\   Hence we obtain the
recurrence relation as,  
:math:`q_{n+1}=xq_n- (\frac{n^2}{4n^2-1})q_{n-1}`\ - :math:`p(x)` is a
monic polynomial of degree :math:`n`.   Let :math:`p_n(x)` be the set of
all polynomials upto degree :math:`n`.   We know that :math:`p_n(x)` is
a vector space of dimension :math:`n+1`.   Let
{:math:`q_i(x): i=0,1,2,...,n`} be the set of Legendre polynomials.  
Define the inner product for :math:`x \in [-1,1]` in :math:`p_n(x)` by,
:math:`<f,g> = \int_{-1}^1 f(x)g(x) \,dx`; where
:math:`f(x),g(x) \in p_n(x)` .   Then {:math:`q_i(x): i=0,1,2,...,n`}
forms an orthogonal set of vectors :math:`\implies` This set is a basis
(As there are exactly :math:`n+1` elements).   Then, for any
:math:`p(x) \in p_n(x)`,
:math:`p(x) = \sum_{i=0}^n \frac{<p(x),q_i(x)>}{<q_i(x),q_i(x)>} q_i(x)`
.  
:math:`\implies \lVert p(x) \rVert_2^2 = \int_{-1}^1 |p(x)|^2 \,dx = \int_{-1}^1 ( \sum_{i=0}^n \frac{<p(x),q_i(x)>}{<q_i(x),q_i(x)>}q_i(x))^2 \,dx`
.   Now since, :math:`\int_{-1}^1 q_i(x)q_j(x) \,dx = 0` for
:math:`i \neq j` .   :math:`\implies` :math:`\lVert p(x) \rVert_2^2`
will be minimized only if :math:`p(x)=q_n(x)`, because in this case all
:math:`<q_n,q_i> = 0`, for :math:`i \neq n` .   Therefore,
:math:`\lVert p(x) \rVert` is minimum :math:`\implies`
:math:`p(x)=q_n(x)`, (proved) .

**:math:`3.`**\ The Chebyshev polynomials of the first kind are defined
as,     :math:`T_n(x)=cos(n arccos(x))`\ - The exponents of :math:`x`
with nonzero coefficients in :math:`T_n` are alternatively odd and even
and the degrre of the polynomial is equals to n.   Now we have to prove
that, :math:`T_{n+1}=2xT_n-T_{n-1}`\   We will prove this by
Mathematical induction.   The statement is clearly true for :math:`n=0`
& :math:`n=1`.   Let us show that it also holds for :math:`n=1`,
assuming that it is true for :math:`n` & :math:`n-1`.Let us assume that
n\ :math:`n` is even, the case when :math:`n` is odd is similar.   The
polynomial :math:`T_{n-1}(x)` has all exponents of x as odd, by
assumption.The polynomial :math:`T_n(x)` has all exponents of x as odd,
also by assumption.   Then, :math:`T_n(x)=2xT_{n-1}(x)-T_{n-2}(x)`, by
definition for :math:`n>1`.   :math:`\therefore`
:math:`T_{n+1}(x)=2xT_{n}(x)-T_{n-1}(x)`\ =\ :math:`2x`\ (evenpower)-oddpower=oddpower.
  Hence, :math:`T_{n+1}=2xT_n-T_{n-1}` is true for all n.[Proved] -
Again this is due to recursion formula.We see that the statement is true
for :math:`n=1` & :math:`n=2`.   Then,
:math:`T_{2+1}=T_3(x)=2xT_2(x)-T_1(x)=2^{2-1}xT_2(x)-T_1(x)`\   The
effect of this is to multiply the leading coefficient by :math:`2`.The
rest of the polynomials are constructed likewise, and we always multiply
the lead coefficient by :math:`2` and at :math:`T_n` the lead
coefficient will be :math:`2^{n-1}`\ - All zeros of :math:`T_{n+1}(x)`
are in the interval :math:`[-1,1]`.   :math:`T_{n+1}(x)=0`\  
Substitute :math:`x=\cos\theta` in the chebyshev polynomial, we get,  
:math:`T_{n+1}(\cos\theta)=\cos(n+1)\theta=0`\   :math:`\implies`
:math:`(n+1)\theta_k=\frac{\pi}{2}+k\pi`, :math:`k \in \{0,1,....n\}`\
  :math:`\implies` :math:`\theta_k=\frac{(2k+1)}{2(n+1)}\pi`\  
:math:`\therefore`
:math:`x_k=\cos\theta_k=\cos(\frac{2k+1}{2(n+1)}\pi)`,
:math:`k \in \{0,1,....n\}`\ - Since, :math:`\cos x` is a periodic
function,   :math:`T_n \in [-1,1]` with :math:`T_n(x_k)=0`, for
:math:`x_k=\{\cos (\frac{2k+1}{2n+2}\pi), k \in \{0,1...,n\}\}`\  
:math:`\therefore` :math:`T_n(x)` alternates in :math:`[-1,1]` for
exactly :math:`(n+1)` times. - We scale the lead coefficients to
:math:`1` of the Chebyshev polynomials, get  
:math:`|(x-x_0)(x-x_1)....(x-x_n)|=\frac{|T_{n+1}(x)|}{2^n}`,
[let,:math:`x_0,x_1,...x_n` be zeros of :math:`T_{n+1}(x)`]   Now,
:math:`T_{n+1}(x)=cos(narccosx)\leq 1`\   :math:`\therefore`
:math:`|\prod_{k=0}^n(x-x_k)|\leq \frac{1}{2^n}, \forall x \in [-1,1]`
